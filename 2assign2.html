1A. SELECTED METHODOLOGY: QUALITATIVE RISK ASSESSMENT

Justifications:

Familiarity and Simplicity: This uses a simple and intuitive approach to identify risks based on the severity and likelihood of their occurrence, which is suitable for a business with simple computer systems.

Resource Availability: The business is small and has limited staff, so a qualitative approach can be effectively implemented without needing specialised risk assessment skills.

Relevance: It focuses on the most visible risks (theft, damage, data loss on single devices, etc.), which are more applicable to a business of this scale and nature.

Key Steps:

Identify Assets: Physical inventory, warehouse management system (old computer with spreadsheet), sales computer, wireless network.

Identify Threats: Theft, data loss, physical damage, unauthorised access (cyber and physical).

Identify Vulnerabilities: Lack of data backups, outdated software, minimal cybersecurity measures.

Assess Risks: Using a risk matrix to prioritise risks based on their potential impact and likelihood.

1B. THREAT MODELLING TECHNIQUE USED IS STRIDE.

Spoofing: An attacker makes a fake email to order goods.

             Qualitative risk assessment: likelihood: high; impact: high

 

Tampering: An attacker hacks a shop's computers through WIFI and then amends payment information.

             Qualitative risk assessment: likelihood: high; impact: extremely high

 

Repudiation: An attacker clears the shop's computer system and security logs.

             Qualitative risk assessment: likelihood: medium impact: extremely high

 

Information Disclosure: A staff member steals customers' private information and exposes it to others.

             Qualitative risk assessment: likelihood: extremely high; impact: extremely high

 

Denial of Service: An attacker launches multiple simultaneous large requests to access WIFI, which causes the network to crash.

             Qualitative risk assessment: likelihood: high; impact: high

 

Elevation of Privilege: A staff member logs in as a general user and then gains the right to privilege.

             Qualitative risk assessment: likelihood: low; impact: high

1C. THESE MITIGATING STRATEGIES ARE CREATED IN CONSIDERATION OF ISO27001, NIST AND PCI-DSS AND ARE RISK-BASED.

Access control: appropriate controls, policies, and procedures would be established for access, account management, access enforcement, information flow enforcement, etc. Appropriate segregation of duties and the concept of least privilege will be enforced. Unsuccessful log-ins would be recorded.

Intrusion detection and prevention systems will be installed, the website will be monitored, and firewalls will be installed to maintain data security.

Perimeter firewalls shall be installed between wireless networks and other sensitive information networks. Firewall and router rule sets policies are reviewed every six months.

Secured payment gateway through which transactions to be submitted to financial networks for authorisation and settlement be installed.

Disaster Recovery Planning: maintain adequate procedures and information to ensure a successful disaster recovery operation.

Periodically apply patches for malware/antivirus signature updates, critical system bug fixes, etc.

Encryption protocols and encryption key management will be employed.

Compliance security audits and penetration tests would be performed regularly.

Individual Project: Executive Summary

In this essay, the food quality and supply chain problems for the pet food company would be first analyzed with the TOPSIS method and the solution would be found with online software. Secondly, the summary of the results and the recommendations on the quality and supply chain problems would be presented. Lastly, the disaster recovery and cloud vendor lock-in issues would be discussed.

1.a. The Technique for Order Preference by Similarity to an Ideal Solution (TOPSIS), a type of multi-criteria decision making (MCDM) methods, is selected. Justification for the TOPSIS method includes its “simplicity, rationality, comprehensibility, good computational efficiency and ability to measure the relative performance for each alternative in a simple mathematical form.” (Roszkowska, 2011) The online software company called onlineoutput.com’s web-based software has been used to perform the actual calculations.

1.b. For the criteria in food quality, it is taken from a study done by Greda (2009):

3 Inappropriate selection and quality of packaging materials 0.1400
4 Instable quality of the produced goods 0.6741

For the criteria in the supply chain, it is taken from website called The Geography of Transport Systems by Dr. Jean-Paul Rodrigue (2024). The probabilities have been adjusted from the original source:

For both quality and supply chain, the alternatives represent different combinations of resources/efforts spent on different criteria (issues). For example, in quality, alternative #1 consists of 1 for criterion #1, 2 for criterion #2, 3 for criterion #3, 4 for criterion #4; that actually means 10% of the company’s resources/efforts to be spent on issue of quality dealing with difficulties to buy raw materials with specific parameters, 20% of the company’s resources/efforts to be spent on dealing with irregularity of supply of raw materials, 30% spent on dealing with inappropriate selection and quality of packaging materials, 40% spent on dealing with instable quality of the produced goods.

Another example, in supply chain alternative #2 consists of 1 for criterion #1, 3 for criterion #2, 5 for criterion #3, 1 for criterion #4; that actually means 10% of the company’s resources/efforts to be spent on supply chain dealing with political instability, 30% of the company’s resources/efforts to be spent on dealing with trade restrictions, 50% spent on dealing with demand shocks, 10% spent on dealing with price volatility.

1.c. For quality, the result is that alternative 1 (10% of resources/efforts for this issue spent on difficulty with raw materials, 20% on irregularity in supply, 30% on problems with packaging, 40% on instable quality problem) ranks highest and should be chosen.

For supply chain, the result is that alternative 2 (10% of resources/efforts for this issue spent on political instability, 30% on trade restrictions, 50% on demand shocks, 10% on price volatility) ranks highest.

3. Disaster recovery strategy and vendor lock-in

Putting into place a business continuity / disaster recovery (DR) strategy

Firstly, the pet food company would perform a risk assessment since it needs to understand the unique set of threats and vulnerabilities specific to this company so that it can likely to succeed in implementing an effective DR strategy. (Snedaker, 2013, p. 451)

Secondly, the company would perform an impact analysis on itself. It needs to quantify the impact of an unplanned outages to the company’s bottom line or reputation, then it would proceed to determine the DR requirements, knowing the correct budget for implementing a recovery strategy. (Snedaker, 2013, p. 451)

Thirdly, it needs to determine its DR requirements. Requirements such as MTD, WRT, RTO and RPO need to be fully understood for all IT systems and data before deciding on an appropriate recovery strategy. The company needs to know what the real requirements are, otherwise it will fail at DR planning and spend too much or too little on protecting its IT assets. (Snedaker, 2013, p. 452)

Fourthly, for reason given below in the next section, online, specifically cloud based, DR strategy is engaged and the company can and should therefore put in place a robust DR plan, with the involvement of the cloud service provider (CSP). Since the pet food company’s critical operations would be dependent on cloud-based platforms, it needs to make sure a disruption in cloud services will not impact the operational resilience. Deficiencies identified would need to be addressed as soon as possible. The pet food company should also have comprehensive understanding of its CSP’s resilience capabilities, including the CSP’s contingency plans and procedures. The pet food company should also ensure these plans and procedures are subject to regular drills and effective testing. (Hong Kong Monetary Authority, 2022)

The DR solution that would lose no more than 1 minute of data loss

As it turns out, the best on premise DR solution would take minutes, many, many minutes of RTO+RPO, which is not acceptable to the investor, and therefore, the only plausible solution candidates can be found online, in the cloud.

Since the Microsoft Azure documentation is found to be wanting by this student, the primary focus would therefore concentrate on AWS services. The investor makes the job of evaluation of candidates very simple for this student by requiring no more than 1 minute of data loss. There is only one candidate left standing to do that job, namely, “Multi-site active/active”, or multiple active sites. The next candidate in the AWS world is the “Warm standby”, which is unacceptable to the investor because it has a RPO/RTO in “minutes”, which is much longer than the required “one minute”, whereas “Multi-site active/active” has a RPO/RTO of “real time” or “zero down time” and “near zero data loss”.

Chosen platform to host the solution

The actual architecture is such that the pet shop would be running its IT services in the AWS cloud “active” in multiple regions; therefore, at least two regions. “This approach is the most complex and costly approach to disaster recovery, but it can reduce your recovery time to near zero for most disasters with the correct technology choices and implementation.” (AWS Documentation)

Since the workload is running in more than one region, it is not a failover scenario. Regular backups are still required and would be tested regularly. Both Amazon Route 53 and AWS Global Accelerator services would enable the definition of policies that determine which users go to which active regional endpoint. Asynchronous data replication with this strategy enables near-zero RPO. AWS services like Amazon Aurora global database use dedicated infrastructure that makes the pet shop’s databases entirely available to serve its application, and can replicate to up to five secondary Region with typical latency of under a second. (AWS Documentation)

Advice on vendor lock-in

Well-informed decision making

According to research performed by Opara-Martins, Sahandi, and Tian (2016), it would appear that “the vetting process for selecting vendors is a critical aspect for effective cloud migration with minimized risk of lock-in.” Also, the pet food company should “focus on constantly monitoring any development or changes in the cloud that may impact data security or hinder interoperability and portability.”

Standards and cloud-based solutions

According to Opara-Martins, Sahandi, and Tian (2016), cloud service providers are aware that standardization in the clouds would let cloud customers easier to leave. “Many cloud providers are concerned with the loss of customer that may come with standardization initiatives which may flatten profits, and do not regard the solution favorable.” These authors therefore suggests that it is up to businesses to be aware that decisions to adopt or move resources to the cloud requires adequate risk analysis for potential of lock-ins. In other words, businesses need to think very, very carefully before they sign the contract to get on the cloud.

Stakeholder engagement

The Quixy editorial team (2024) suggests that understanding the particular risks related to a particular cloud service provider lock-in calls for stakeholder engagement. Dialogue leaded by the leaders must get all other stakeholders in the organization to realize the benefits and risks of getting on the cloud and cloud lock-ins. Also, “in making technical choices, architects and technical groups need to remember the commercial enterprise implications.” In any case, they need to think very carefully before getting on the cloud.

Consider upgrading before migration

Is moving to the cloud absolutely necessary? Is it possible to upgrade and improve on the existing IT infrastructures in the company? (Quixy editorial team, 2024) The pet food company needs to ask such questions.

Develop a multi-cloud approach

Using two or more public clouds simultaneously is becoming increasingly popular, according to the Quixy editorial team (2024). “By using this approach, companies cannot only keep away from seller lock-in but also take advantage of the quality pricing, functions, and infrastructure components to be had throughout cloud carriers.”

In conclusion, hopefully, the MCDM problems have been solved with satisfactory solution. Moreover, the disaster recovery and cloud vendor lock-in issues could be resolved.

STEP 5: Calculate the relative closeness degree of alternatives to the ideal solution

In this step, the relative closeness degree of each alternative to the ideal solution is obtained by the following formula. If the relative closeness degree has value near to 1, it means that the alternative has shorter distance from the positive ideal solution and longer distance from the negative ideal solution.

$$C_{i} = \frac{d_{i}^{-}}{(d_{i}^{+} + d_{i}^{-})}\ \ \ \ ,\ \ \ i = 1,\ \ldots,\ m$$
