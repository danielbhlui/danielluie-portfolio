Unit 5 e-Portfolio Component

3) Case Study 3: The Dublin Mint Office Limited

The DPC received a complaint on 13 October 2017 from an individual who had received two marketing telephone calls that same day, one targeted at him and one at his son, from The Dublin Mint Office Limited. The caller in each case had attempted to sell commemorative coins. In his complaint, the complainant explained that he had registered online a few months earlier with the company for an online offer on his own behalf and on behalf of his son, providing the same telephone contact number for both of them during the online registration process. The complainant stated that he ticked the marketing opt-out box during that online registration process.

During the course of the DPC’s investigation, The Dublin Mint Office Limited admitted that it had made the marketing telephone calls. It explained that when the complainant supplied his telephone number during the online application process in May 2017 the order form had only offered an opt-in option to receive marketing mails and emails. The company confirmed that the complainant had not selected the opt-in option and he was therefore marked as opt-out for marketing mails and emails only. The company explained that a gap in the system in place at the time only allowed for an opt-in to marketing mails and emails but that it was not an opt-out for telesales. As a result, the complainant’s details were included in a list for a follow-up telesales call. The company informed the DPC that it had written to the complainant to apologise for the inconvenience caused to him and to his son by its inadvertent mistake.

The DPC had previously issued a warning to The Dublin Mint Office Limited in September 2017 concerning other complaints which had been made to the DPC concerning unsolicited marketing communications by the company. The DPC therefore decided to prosecute The Dublin Mint Office Limited. At Dublin Metropolitan District Court on 14 May 2018 the company pleaded guilty to two charges in relation to both marketing telephone calls. It also agreed to cover the DPC’s prosecution costs. In lieu of a conviction and fine, the Court applied Section 1(1) of the Probation of Offenders Act.

1. How do Goerlandt et al (2017) suggest that the validity of QRA approaches can be validated? What did they posit was the most effective approach?

2. Which techniques did Hugo et al (2018) should be applied to project management? What were their recommendations to increase the use of QR analysis in Projects?

3. The last paper reviews various Multi-Criteria Decision Methods (MCDMs) and considered the relative accuracy and validity of the techniques. Which did they find was the most accurate of the methods compared? What were the failings of the general TOPSIS approach?

Answers:

  1. The conceptual validity school defines 4 validity criteria:

V1. The degree to which the produced numbers are accurate compared to the underlying true risk. V2. The degree to which the assigned subjective probabilities adequately describe the assessor’s uncertainties of the unknown quantities considered. V3. The degree to which the epistemic uncertainty assessments are complete. V4. The degree to which the analysis addresses the right quantities (ﬁctional quantities or observable events).

In the scientific foundations school, risk realists believe risk is a physical property that can be characterized by objective facts.

In the pragmatic validity of QRA school, the accuracy claim means that the aggregate estimate of total system risk be sufficiently accurate and precise to allow correct decision making. The cost-effective usefulness claim suggests that performing QRA provides a safety benefit. The usefulness claim means that the application of QRA helps designers to make safer systems and/or decision makers to make better decisions.

Goerlandt and others believes more research is needed is needed for instance with respect to the effectiveness of the proposed methods for the different identiﬁed uses of QRA, and which processes, techniques and social structures support their successful application.

  1. Hugo and others believe that project management tools and schedule and cost management tools should be applied to project management.

With regards to their recommendations to increase the use of QR analysis in Projects, they recommend that:

a. Improve individuals’ risk management competence via training, exposure, etc.

b. Align the parent organization’s approach to risk management with projects, and strive to improve the organization’s maturity levels in project and risk management processes.

c. Make available the required resources, both human and software, to carry out risk management, both for qualitative and quantitative risk management.

3. According to Çelikbilek and Tüysüz, AHP and MOORA are much more accurate and valid technique.

There are 3 criticisms to TOPSIS, the first is the rank reversal phenomenon which is valid for AHP and TOPSIS methods. Rank reversal is related to the change in the ranking of alternatives when a criterion or an alternative is added or dropped. Another important criticism is about the ranking index of the TOPSIS. Another criticism or disadvantage of the TOPSIS is related to the correlation between criteria. Since the TOPSIS method uses Euclidean distance, which does not take into consideration the correlation, the results are affected due to the information overlap.

Reference

Goerlandt, F., Khakzad, N. and Reniers, G. (2017). Validity and validation of safety-related quantitative risk analysis: A review. Safety Science, 99, pp.127–139.

Hugo, F.D., Pretorius, L. & Benade, S.J. (2018) Some Aspects of the use and Usefulness of Quantitative Risk Analysis Tools in Project Management. South African Journal of Industrial Engineering, 29(4).

Unit 12 Seminar

The Trend of Increasing awareness of the importance of cybersecurity in the higher level of organizations

In the US, there is increased board of directors’ involvement on the issue of cybersecurity because of the increased penalty associated with the lack of actions dealing with cybersecurity.

In the US in 2022, there were 83% of companies suffered from multiple data breaches, with the average expense of $9.44 million. It represents a 600% over the past ten years. The cost of cybersecurity incidents to companies and investors was rising at an increasing rate. The financial impact of data breaches on organizations has increased by 15 percent in the past three years. (kovrr.com)

A recent rule was passed on July 26, 2023 by the Security and Exchange Commission to require publicly traded US companies and other private issuers to report “material cybersecurity incidents” within four days of positively identifying an event. These companies also are required to make annual disclosures describing their cybersecurity governance and the impacts of previous incidents. (sec.gov)

The rule went into effect late last year and gave a boost to cybersecurity risk management practices in companies by increasing accountability and protecting investors better by ensuring they are aware of risk exposure.

In the EU, there is a NIS2 directive that outlines more explicitly corporate responsibility. It aims to achieve a high common level of cybersecurity across the European Union. To comply with the new Directive, organizations must take measures to minimize cyber risks. These measures include incident management, stronger supply chain security, enhanced network security, better access control, and encryption. The directive primarily affects large companies.

Recently, Australian Securities and Investments Commission chairman Joe Longo warned boards of the penalties should they fail to make reasonable investments in cybersecurity.

To summarize, boards of directors in many countries are increasingly aware of cybersecurity and the penalties of not taking care of it in their companies.
