Collaborative Discussion Forum Summaries

Summary Post #1 (Unit 3)

According to the authors Kovaite, Sumakaris, Stankeviciene, and Korsakiene (2019), Industry 4.0 represents “a new paradigm of global technological developments in industry, society and business.” A number of technological drivers such as IoT, big data and AI appears to have big impact and disrupts on business models in almost any industry, which makes “the physical and geographical boundaries of industry disappear” and the new “opportunities in newly established supply chains provide possibilities for SMEs to compete in international markets.”

With opening up of new opportunities there comes a number of risks, especially cybersecurity risks. Firstly, “industrial espionage and industrial sabotage are very real concerns for industries where sensitive and proprietary data could be exposed or even destroyed. Another example (of risks) is the deliberate disruption of manufacturing processes by malicious hackers to cause damage to manufacturing entities.” (Prinsloo, Sinha, and von Solms, 2019)

In terms of supports or contradictions the points made in the cited study, Muller and Voigt (2018) finds that, for SMEs there exists a lower perceived impact of the IIoT for their respective enterprise. Also, Muller and Voigt expect that Industry 4.0 might be designed more towards the requirements of larger enterprises. Its benefits therefore might be harder to capture by SMEs.

Daniel Malok in his peer response argues that Industry 4.0 can increase SMEs competitiveness in various ways. I welcome a contrary viewpoint and I find it persuasive.

Mr Necat is concerned with the consequences of the widely use of IOT devices; especially, the invasion of privacy. I accept there would be likely invasion of privacy and it is up to the society to put in effective controls to reduce risk of abuse to an acceptable level.

Tyrone Lim has provided good summaries of various authors’ viewpoints and I accept them.

 

Reference

Kovaite, K. Sumakaris, P. Stankeviciene, J and Korsakiene, R. (2019) Industry 4.0 as the driving force of SME internationalization: A case of Lithuania. Sciendo, pp 1-15.

Prinsloo, J. Sinha, S. and von Solms, B. (2019) A Review of Industry 4.0 Manufacturing Process Security Risks’, in Norberto, L., De Lacalle, L. and Posada, J. (eds.) New Industry 4.0 Advances in Industrial IoT and Visual Computing for Manufacturing Processes: Volume II. Applied Sciences, pp. 1-32.

Muller, J. M. and Voigt, K. (2018) Sustainable Industrial Value Creation in SMEs: A Comparison between Industry 4.0 and Made in China 2025. International Journal of Precision Engineering and Manufacturing Green Technology Vol. 5, No. 5, pp. 659-670





Summary Post #2 (Unit 9)

Spring and others (2021) are not holding back when they criticize CVSS. Firstly, “the CVSS scoring algorithm is not justified, either formally or empirically.” They believe the CVSS formula is not properly justified and too many workarounds, temporary fix and other issues make CVSS unusable. Secondly, the CVSS is not born being a risk score per se, but is a vulnerability score. That in Spring and others’ eyes is a major sin. And they write in length of the consequences of this as CVSS’ failure to account for context, failure to account for material consequences of the vulnerability and being operational scoring problems.

I agree with Spring and others that the CVSS formula is not justified and I agree their suggestion that “adequate user studies should be conducted to understand how organizations use CVSS in their risk assessments today.” Also, I believe CVSS should be used as it is, a vulnerability indicator and should not be used as a risk indicator, as it has never intended to be.

This student believes that the alternative that will replace CVSS 3 would be CVSS 4.0. This is because in questionnaire and open feedback comments as cited by Wunder and others, “users are aware of problems associated with CVSS, but that these do not outweigh its advantages. Over 70% clearly agreed to use CVSS in the future.”

I note Thomas Smith’s point that it is important to distinguish between vulnerability and risk. It looks like many professionals are not getting it.

I agree with Chi Ming that CVSS is not a failure. Far from it, I think CVSS would be hanging around for a long time.

 

Reference

Spring, J. M. Hatleback, E. Householder, A. Manion, A. and Shick, D. (2021)“Time to Change the CVSS?” IEEE Security & Privacy, vol. 19, pp. 74–78.

Wunder, J. Kurtz, A. Eichenmüller, C. Gassmann, F. and Benenson, Z. (2024) ‘Shedding Light on CVSS Scoring Inconsistencies: A User-Centric Study on Evaluating Widespread Security Vulnerabilities’, Proceedings of the IEEE Symposium on Security and Privacy
