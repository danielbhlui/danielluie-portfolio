Reflections

The last assignment of this course requires me to reflect upon critically the events as well as my learnings during this course.

The Security and Risk Management process based on what you have learned in this module

If there is anything that I have learned during this course, it would be the risk management process (i.e. Asset identification, Risk analysis, Vulnerability identification, Probability and impact analysis (qualitative and quantitative risk analysis), and Developing mitigation strategies). After being repeatedly drilled into me by Mr. Necat, I believe I can apply the process very satisfactorily to a given risk situation. For example, I have been practicing to apply the risk management framework to a couple of problems in this course.

Of course, there are also a lot of other things I have learned. Those would be described in the last section of this paper.

My individual contributions to the team activities

I am responsible for the following parts in the essay:

1c. A list of potential mitigations to the identified risks and threats (for the stand-alone shop).

2b. A list of proposed changes that form the basis of the digitalization process/ transformation

2e. A list of potential mitigations to the identified risks and threats (for the digitalized shop).

Also, I serve as the coordinator and editor besides writing my parts. Daniel Malok also edits.

My experience as a member of a development team

In our group project, we have been assigned to do a relatively short risk identification report. I suppose due to the fact that it is relatively short, it has not too much of pressure on us. In addition to writing my part, I have taken the initiative to take on the coordinator and editor roles. At the end of the project, one member of our team actually thanks my leadership. I must say that makes my day. I also am glad to report that there is no drama in this group, unlike some ugly situations I have been through before. I suppose communication is the key. Another thing that I have learned in group work is the need to conduct proper time management. Some team members are clearly motivated only when the deadline is looming, in order not to destroy rapport, I can only give them a gentle push. That is another instance where good communications are important right there.

The impact on my professional/personal development

Firstly, I must admit 12 weeks have zoomed by just like that. If I could do this course over again, I wish I could have performed tighter grip on time management. I would definitely keep that in mind before my next course, network security.

I have previously studied financial risk management (FRM). I have been wondering if financial risk management and cybersecurity risk management would be more like the same or different. It turns out they are more different than similar and also the skill sets required are very different. Although both rely to a big extent on qualitative and quantitative methods, the contents and look and feels are very different. Before taking this course, I have been naïve to think that the two are vastly similar, boy, was I wrong? In FRM, the typical scenario is to figure out whether a risk is too cheap or too expensive, whereas in the cyber world, all risks are down side risks only and are subject to risk management. Technical skills and commercial awareness often go together.

In thread modeling, the students have learned a variety of techniques such as STRIDE and PASTA. I must admit that it is news to me that there is a security dimension towards software development. In undergraduate computer science studies, I have been taught to make the program to work, but was never told about this security aspect. So now that I feel like it is like an eye opening for me to learn of this security aspect in software engineering. I am pleased to learn of this important aspect of security. From now on, I will take security seriously when writing a program, if possible.

In unit 5, we have learned about the security and risk standards. I am a little disappointed ISO 27000 and nist only got discussed in the lecturecast, but not in the readings. Due to the requirements in the Hong Kong job market, I would definitely want to know more about the two. It is very important to be commercially aware if I want to obtain a return on this degree.

In unit 7, we have been exposed to different quantitative methods and their implementations. While I am not about to yell out loud that “math is hard,” I must also acknowledge that quant really is the most difficult component in this course. I am therefore glad that I have found the resilience in me that makes me successful in travelling this journey. The proof is that I have successfully completed the highly quantitative individual assignment.

Before learning the skills taught in this course, I was dealing with multicriteria decision making (MCDM) in a highly intuitive manner, which could more likely let me to arrive at an incorrect decision. Now that I have learned quantitative assessment and MCDM, I know that the decision I make is more grounded on science and on more firmed footings.

In unit 9 and 10, we have learned about business continuity and disaster recovery plans. I am thankful that the lecturecast has given me the necessary information required (i.e. active-active) to successfully complete the individual assignment. I have also performed adequate critical thinking and analysis, as well as problem-solving in completing the assignment. But without that one piece of information, it would be very difficult for me to do well in that assignment, even if I have done an extensive amount of research for that assignment.

In unit 11 and 12, we have learned about the trends and future of cybersecurity risk management. To this student, it seems a little remote. This is not to say that they are not important. Of course they are. Just that they still seem a little speculative to me. Still, I must say that I am aware more about the future trends in security management after studying these two units.
